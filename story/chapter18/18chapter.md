# \[Chapter 18\] Ashes

The knife slipped before Delia realized her hands were shaking.

Blood welled across her palm against pale skin. She stared, watched it pool in the creases of her hand, deeper than expected. The bread lay half-sliced on the cutting board.

"You're bleeding."

Amber's voice behind her. Delia hadn't heard her come down the stairs. She turned, pressing her palm against her apron, and saw the girl standing in the kitchen doorway. Ten years old. Dark hair. Fuji's daughter.

"I'm fine." The words automatic.

Amber stepped forward, reaching for the dish towel. "Let me---"

"Don't!" Sharper than Delia meant. Louder. Amber stopped mid-reach, hand hovering. "I can't even..." Delia's voice cracked. She looked down at her palm, at the black dress draped over the chair—the funeral in two hours—at this child. "I can't even make breakfast without---"

Stephen would have known what to do. Stephen always knew what to do. But Stephen was dead, and his funeral was in two hours, and this girl—this *child*—was standing in Professor Oak's kitchen because her father had put Delia's name on a birth certificate.

"If you weren't here I wouldn't---" Delia stopped herself, but not soon enough. She saw Amber's hands drop to her sides. Behind Amber, in the living room, Ash's head appeared over the back of the couch.

"I can't look at you," Delia heard herself say, quieter now but somehow worse. "Without seeing him burning."

Amber didn't move. Didn't speak. Just stood there with that careful, blank expression that made Delia's chest ache.

Professor Oak's footsteps in the hallway. "Delia?" His voice gentle, like speaking to a wounded Pokemon. "Let me help with that hand."

She let him guide her to the bathroom, let him run water over the cut and wrap it in gauze.

"The funeral is in two hours," Oak said quietly, not quite looking at her as he secured the bandage. "Can you do this?"

Delia nodded.

"I'll talk to her," Oak added, and left her sitting on the edge of the bathtub, her bandaged hand throbbing.

The white tiles blurred. The bathroom was too bright, too clean, too much like the hospital where they'd told her Stephen was missing.

Back to the ruins.

\-\[v.v\]-

The ash still hung in the air three days after the fire.

Delia pulled the mask higher on her face as she moved through what used to be her living room. The walls were gone. The roof was gone. Just charred support beams and blackened frame. Ten years.

Professor Oak walked a few feet ahead. He'd stop every few feet, crouch down, examine something.

"The fire patterns here..." He gestured at a section of blackened floorboards. "Magmar's coordination was deliberate. See how the burn marks radiate from multiple points?" His finger traced lines through the ash. "Not random. Orchestrated."

She was staring at what used to be the kitchen, at the twisted metal frame of their stove. Stephen had cooked breakfast there every Sunday morning. Pancakes for Ash. Coffee for her. He'd sing off-key while he cooked, some old Kantonian folk song his father used to hum.

Her foot caught on something. She looked down.

A melted picture frame. The photo inside was nothing but curled black paper, but she knew which one it had been. Ash at six months old, all gums and squealing joy. The frame had sat on the mantle. She'd dusted it every week.

She bent down and picked it up anyway. The metal was still warm.

"These scorch marks," Oak continued to her left. "Electabuzz tried to interfere. You can see where the electrical burns disrupt the fire patterns. But Fuji..." He trailed off, then stood up. "He orchestrated this. Every step planned."

Delia set the melted frame down carefully and moved deeper into the wreckage. Her hands found things without conscious decision. A pot with no handle. A drawer pull. Stephen's hammer, the wooden grip burned away but the metal head intact.

"Delia."

Oak's voice was different now. Serious enough to make her look up.

He was crouched near what used to be the hall closet, a small metal box in his hands. Fireproof lockbox. They'd kept important documents in there—birth certificates, insurance papers, things that seemed important before.

Oak opened it. Papers inside, barely singed. He pulled out a document, read it, froze.

"Delia." He looked up at her. "You should see this."

She crossed the ash-covered floor and took the paper from his hands.

Birth certificate. Amber Fuji.

Her own name on the "Mother" line.

"This isn't just forged paper." Oak's hands tightened on the lockbox. "Whoever did this... they're in League databases." He paused. "Medical records. School registration. Everything."

Delia's fingers tightened on the certificate, crinkling the edges. Her name. On a document for the child of the man who'd murdered her husband.

"If Team Rocket can create false identities in League systems..." Oak didn't finish the thought. He looked deeply troubled. "Lance needs to see this. After the funeral, I have to go to Indigo Plateau."

The words took a moment to register. "You're leaving?"

"I have to show him the evidence." Oak gestured at the ruins around them, at the certificate in her hands. "The drugs Fuji used. This level of database access. If they can hack government systems..."

He'd be gone. For weeks, probably. Chasing evidence and Team Rocket connections while she was left here with---

Delia looked back down at the certificate. At her name where it had no right to be.

Her voice came out small. "What am I supposed to do?"

Oak wouldn't meet her eyes. "The League's working to prove the intrusion. But it could take months." He paused. "I know this isn't fair to you."

Fair. The word felt wrong. Before this certificate, Amber would have just gone to League foster care. The system would have handled it. Delia wouldn't have had to DO anything.

But now she was legally Amber's mother. To get rid of her, Delia would have to actively choose to put her up for adoption. Her own "child."

It shouldn't have felt different. But it did.

She folded the certificate once, twice, crumpled it unconsciously. Then she smoothed it back out. It was Amber's identity. She shouldn't---

She crumpled it again and shoved it in her jacket pocket.

"I'll be back in a few weeks," Oak said. His hand was already on a piece of charred wood, steadying himself to stand. "We'll find a solution."

Delia nodded.

Stephen's wedding ring, intact in a pile of ash where their bedroom used to be. Delia slipped it into her other pocket, feeling the warm metal against her palm.

A baking sheet, warped from heat.

One of Ash's shoes.

They walked back to Oak's house in silence, Delia carrying a small cardboard box of salvaged metal and ash. The certificate crumpled in her pocket.

She could see Oak's house ahead, light in the windows. And through the window---Amber, sitting at the kitchen table. Watching TV with Ash, or maybe reading. Just... there.

Delia's chest tightened. Her breath caught. Had to stop walking for a moment, hand pressed against her ribs.

Five more days until the funeral. Then Oak would leave.

And she'd be alone with this.

\-\[v.v\]-

The cemetery grass was wet from morning rain, and Delia's shoes sank into the soft earth with each step.

She stood at the front with Ash pressed against her side, his small hand gripping her dress too tight. The casket sat in front of them, polished wood. People stood in clusters. She recognized faces—Officer Jenny's uniform, Stephen's coworkers, neighbors—but their names floated just out of reach.

The pastor was saying something. Words about rest and peace and loving husband and father. The words reached her, but she couldn't hold onto them. Rest. Peace. Loving husband.

Her hand found Ash's shoulder. Held on. He was shaking, or maybe she was.

Across the gathering, Amber stood next to Professor Oak. Not with family. Separate. The girl's eyes focused somewhere past Delia's shoulder, face very still. Delia looked away.

The casket lowered. Someone—Jenny, maybe—touched Delia's arm and said something she didn't catch. She nodded anyway. More people came by. Murmured condolences. She responded with the right words, the automatic ones. "Thank you for coming." "Yes, it's been difficult." "We appreciate it."

Her hands ached from gripping Ash so tight.

When it was over, when the last person had drifted away and only Oak remained, Delia finally let herself breathe. The air tasted like rain and turned earth.

"Come on, sweetheart," she said quietly to Ash. "Let's go home."

Except they couldn't go home. Home was ash and charred beams and melted picture frames.

They rode back to Oak's house in his car, Ash silent in the backseat, Amber in the front passenger seat staring out the window. Delia watched the back of her head. Felt nothing. Or everything.

\-\[v.v\]-

Oak's bags were already packed, sitting by the front door. Sample cases. Documentation folders. Evidence to show Lance.

"I'll call when I can," he said, his hand on the doorknob. "Lance needs to see the evidence." He looked at Delia. "Take care of yourself. And them."

Delia nodded. Her throat had closed.

The door closed behind him.

Delia stood in the entryway for a moment, listening to Oak's car start and pull away. Then she turned to find Ash and Amber both watching her from the living room. Ash's eyes were red-rimmed. Amber's were unreadable.

"I'm going to lie down," Delia heard herself say. "Ash, come on."

She took her son to the guest room and closed the door. Left Amber downstairs alone.

\-\[v.v\]-

Ash finally fell asleep around midnight, his breathing evening out after an hour of nightmares that finally subsided. Delia lay next to him in the narrow guest bed, staring at the ceiling.

She should apologize. The thought circled endlessly: she should go downstairs and tell Amber it wasn't her fault. That the words this morning were wrong. That grief made people say terrible things they didn't mean.

Except she did mean them. That was the horrible truth of it. She couldn't look at Amber without seeing Stephen burning. Couldn't be near her without her chest going tight and her hands starting to shake.

She knew it wasn't Amber's fault. Knew it intellectually, knew it the same way she knew Ash's birthday or her own address—a fact that changed nothing. Knowing didn't change how her body reacted. Didn't change how her throat closed up every time the girl walked into a room.

Tomorrow, Delia thought. She'd talk to her tomorrow. When she'd slept. When the funeral wasn't so fresh. When she could breathe without it hurting.

The house creaked around her. Old wood settling. Or maybe Amber, moving around downstairs.

Delia closed her eyes and tried not to think about the girl alone down there. About the look on her face this morning.

Tomorrow.

The gray light of pre-dawn was just starting to show around the window edges when Delia heard it---the soft click of the front door closing. So quiet she almost missed it.

She lay there for a moment, trying to make sense of the sound. Too early for Oak. Too early for anyone to be leaving.

Unless.

Delia sat up slowly, careful not to wake Ash. Listened to the silence of the house. No footsteps. No movement.

She should get up. Should check. But exhaustion pinned her to the bed, and some part of her—the part that couldn't face another confrontation, another failure—thought maybe it was nothing. Maybe she'd imagined it.

She lay back down.

Closed her eyes.

Didn't get up.

\-\[v.v\]-

Delia woke to sunlight cutting across the bed and Ash still asleep beside her. Her bandaged hand throbbed.

The house was too quiet.

She got up carefully, leaving Ash sleeping, and opened the guest room door. She walked to the borrowed room where Amber had been staying.

The door was open.

The bed was neatly made, hospital corners crisp, sheets tucked tight. The window slightly open, curtain moving in the morning breeze. Everything tidy, like the room had never been used at all.

Except for the note on the pillow.

Delia crossed the room and picked it up. The paper was torn neatly from a notebook. The handwriting was round and careful.

*Thank you for everything. I'm sorry.*

*---Amber*

Delia read it twice. Then a third time.

*I'm sorry.*

Delia's legs felt weak. She sat down on the edge of the bed, still holding the note.

She looked around the room. Empty. The closet door hung open, showing nothing inside. She got up and went downstairs, the note crumpling in her fist.

The kitchen was empty. A few pieces of bread were missing from the loaf on the counter. One of the water bottles from the fridge was gone.

Ditto's pokeball was gone from the coffee table.

She'd really left.

A ten-year-old girl. Alone. With nothing but some bread and a Pokemon that couldn't hold its shape.

Delia stood in the empty kitchen. Morning light filled the windows. She looked down at the note in her hand.

*I'm sorry.*

Oak was gone. Stephen was dead. The house was destroyed. And now Amber was gone too.

Everything Fuji had touched was broken.

Delia smoothed the note out carefully on the counter, then crumpled it again in her fist. Her bandaged hand ached where she'd cut it yesterday. Or was it still bleeding? She couldn't tell anymore.

Through the kitchen window, she could see Route 1 in the distance. Hills and tall grass and wild Pokemon. Somewhere out there, a ten-year-old in the tall grass.

Delia sank into a kitchen chair.

The morning light grew stronger. Ash would wake soon. She'd have to explain. Have to tell him Amber was gone. Have to figure out if she should call Officer Jenny, or wait for Oak to call, or...

She didn't know.

She sat there with the note crumpled in her fist, looking out at Route 1 disappearing into the hills.

Blood had seeped through the gauze on her hand overnight, a dark stain spreading across the white bandage.

She still didn't know if it had stopped bleeding.
